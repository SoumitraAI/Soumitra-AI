This week, BCG published a piece arguing that [AI is turning M&A into a "high-impact learning machine"](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/ai-is-turning-m-and-a-into-a-high-impact-learning-machine) — that dealmaking is shifting from an episodic, experience-driven process into a system where each transaction teaches the next.

I read it with particular interest, because I've recently had the chance to observe a live transaction process from the inside. And what surprised me most was not the complexity of the analysis or the number of advisors involved.

It was how much knowledge gets created during a deal — and how little of it survives.

Financial models are built. Commercial assumptions are challenged. Risks are identified. Management teams are interviewed. Hundreds of pages of analysis are produced across multiple workstreams. Yet once a transaction completes, much of that knowledge becomes surprisingly difficult to access. It sits inside advisor reports, data rooms, spreadsheets, inboxes, and meeting notes. People move on, priorities change, and institutional knowledge slowly fades. The next deal begins, and much of the process starts again.

This isn't primarily a technology problem. It's a learning problem.

And that is why I think BCG's framing is the right one: the most significant impact of AI on M&A may not be automation, but the ability to help organisations learn from every transaction they undertake.

## M&A has always been a knowledge business

At its core, M&A is an exercise in reducing uncertainty. Teams gather information, test assumptions, identify risks, and make decisions with incomplete knowledge. The better the quality of insight, the better the probability of a good decision.

What struck me in practice was how fragmented that insight can become. Legal teams analyse contracts. Financial advisors build models. Commercial teams assess customers and markets. HR evaluates organisational structures and culture. Technology specialists review systems and infrastructure. Each group develops a different perspective on the same business — and those perspectives do not always connect.

In many organisations, insights remain trapped within individual workstreams, individual advisors, or individual team members. Valuable lessons learned during one transaction rarely become part of the institutional memory that informs the next.

This is where AI starts to become genuinely interesting.

## AI is expanding what deal teams can see

Much of the current discussion around AI in M&A focuses on efficiency, and understandably so. AI can review contracts faster, extract information from financial statements, and search large data rooms in seconds. For small deal teams operating under tight deadlines, that creates immediate value.

But the more interesting applications extend beyond speed. In their analysis, BCG's authors describe AI-powered due diligence creating value across four dimensions: accelerating routine work, deepening analysis, broadening the scope of what gets examined, and — most importantly — integrating insights across workstreams.

The first is straightforward: high-volume, repetitive tasks that once took days can be compressed into hours. The second and third matter more. Historically, many diligence exercises were shaped by practical constraints — teams simply didn't have time to investigate every potential risk, so areas like ESG exposure, reputational risk, culture, and intellectual property were reviewed selectively. AI is making it possible to analyse these areas without proportionally increasing cost or timelines, while pattern recognition across large datasets surfaces revenue quality issues, retention trends and operational anomalies earlier than traditional methods allow.

The fourth dimension may prove the most valuable. A contractual obligation identified by legal advisors may affect a financial model. Employee turnover patterns may influence integration planning. Technology dependencies may change assumptions about future investment. These connections have always existed — what's changing is that they're becoming systematically easier to find, rather than depending on the right two people happening to compare notes.

## The real opportunity is institutional memory

While automation receives most of the attention, the larger opportunity lies elsewhere.

Every transaction generates knowledge. Some investment theses prove correct; others don't. Certain risks emerge during diligence; others only become visible after completion. Some integrations exceed expectations, while others struggle despite extensive planning.

Yet much of this learning remains difficult to access. Pipeline information sits in spreadsheets. Relationship history remains in individual inboxes. Deal rationales become buried inside presentations and folders. In effect, organisations repeatedly pay to generate knowledge without consistently retaining it.

AI has the potential to change this equation. Relationship intelligence platforms can map networks and interactions automatically. AI systems can capture diligence findings, investment rationales, negotiation outcomes, and integration lessons in formats that are searchable and reusable. Over time, each transaction can improve the next: sourcing becomes better informed by previous acquisitions, diligence becomes stronger because historical risks surface automatically, and integration planning improves because teams can reference what worked and what failed before.

Instead of treating every transaction as an isolated event, organisations can begin treating transactions as part of a continuous learning system. That, in my view, is the most important shift underway.

## Human judgment becomes more valuable, not less

Despite the excitement, M&A remains fundamentally a human activity.

AI can identify patterns, surface anomalies, summarise information, and generate hypotheses. It cannot negotiate with a founder. It cannot build trust with management teams. It cannot persuade an investment committee or assume responsibility for a final decision. Nor should it.

One real risk is automation bias: the tendency to place excessive trust in outputs simply because a sophisticated system produced them. The strongest deal teams will not use AI as a substitute for judgment — they will use it to focus attention on the questions that matter most, while retaining accountability for the decisions themselves. The competitive advantage will come from combining machine-scale analysis with experienced human judgment.

## What should leaders do today?

For leaders responsible for M&A, the practical challenge is not deciding whether AI matters. It is deciding where to begin.

The most effective organisations appear to be focusing on a few high-impact use cases rather than attempting wholesale transformation: improving how diligence knowledge is captured, creating searchable repositories of deal insights, and building workflows that preserve context long after transactions close. Equally important is treating data generated during transactions as a long-term asset rather than a temporary project output.

The organisations that learn fastest will not necessarily be those that complete the most deals. They will be those that retain and reuse what they learn from every deal they undertake.

## Final thoughts

For decades, competitive advantage in M&A largely came from access to information and experience. AI does not eliminate the importance of either — it changes how they are accumulated.

The firms that benefit most will not necessarily be those with the most advanced models or the largest technology budgets. They will be the organisations that build systems capable of capturing, connecting, and compounding insight across every transaction.

In that sense, the future of M&A is not simply about automation. It is about learning. And AI may prove to be the most powerful institutional memory ever created.
